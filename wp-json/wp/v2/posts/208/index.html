Social engineering is a form of cyberattack that relies on manipulating human psychology rather than exploiting technical vulnerabilities. It is one of the most common and effective ways for hackers to gain unauthorized access to sensitive data, systems or networks.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

In this blog post, we will explain what social engineering is, how it works, and what you can do to prevent it.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

What is social engineering?<\/h2>\n\n\n\n

Definition of social engineering: the art of<\/strong> human hacking that<\/strong> deceive people<\/strong> into providing valuable information or access to that information or resource. The goal of social engineering is to trick someone into performing an action that benefits the attacker, such as clicking on a malicious link, opening an infected attachment, revealing a password, or granting access to a restricted area.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Social engineering works based on the weakness of human nature<\/strong>. We are social creatures who want to be needed, trust people, avoid trouble, and save time. These are all good qualities, but they can also make us vulnerable to deception and manipulation. Social engineers use various techniques to appeal to our emotions, curiosity, greed, or fear, and make us do what they want:<\/p>\n\n\n\n

    \n
  • The desire to be helpful: Most people want to help others, especially if they seem to be in need or in trouble. A social engineer can use this to ask for favors, such as opening a door, clicking a link, or sharing a password.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
  • A tendency to trust people: People tend to believe what others say, especially if they appear to be authoritative, friendly or familiar. A social engineer can use this to impersonate someone else, such as a colleague, a friend, or an authority figure.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
  • The fear of getting into trouble: People may comply with requests that seem to come from a superior, a colleague or a law enforcement officer, even if their disguise are suspicious.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
  • The willingness to cut corners: People may take shortcuts or bypass security procedures to save time or effort, especially if they are under pressure or in a hurry.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

    What are the goals of social engineering?<\/h2>\n\n\n\n

    The goals of social engineering vary depending on the attacker’s motives and objectives. Some common goals are:<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      \n
    • Identity theft: The social engineer wants to steal your personal information and use it for fraudulent purposes, such as opening accounts in your name, applying for loans or credit cards, or making purchases online.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
    • Financial gain: The social engineer wants to access your financial accounts and transfer money to their own account, or use your credit card details to make purchases online.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
    • Data breach: The social engineer wants to access your confidential data and sell it to third parties, such as competitors, hackers<\/a>, or criminals. This could include trade secrets, customer information, employee records, or intellectual property.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
    • Sabotage: The social engineer wants to damage your reputation, business, or operations by deleting or altering your data, disrupting your services, or spreading false information.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
    • Espionage: The social engineer wants to spy on your activities and communications for political, military, or personal reasons such as revenge.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n
